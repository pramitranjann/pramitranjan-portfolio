Mixed Media · 2024

Faces of Power

Key Insight_
“Removing colour and context made the images feel universal. Anyone could be in power.”
Problem_
What does power look like up close?

A mixed media art project exploring how leaders make decisions that impact millions. Using close-up portraiture, interviews, and layered physical materials, the work interrogates privacy, intimacy, and authority.

My Role_
Sole artist across concept, photography, material fabrication, and installation.
Solution_
A multi-part installation where power is constructed, projected, and concealed.

The final work combined Mod Roc masks, acetate lightbox prints, gelli prints, and photograms, each revealing a different way power is performed and hidden.

Pramit Ranjan
01
Research_

Shifting focus from geopolitics to the human actors behind it.

This work extended the South China Sea piece, shifting focus from geopolitics to the human actors behind it. I was interested in what it means to hold power and what gets hidden behind a public face.

Research_
The public face was only the starting point.

The project moved away from policy and toward portraiture, interviews, and physical material. It asked what happens when the distance between viewer and authority collapses.

Challenge_
Close-up portraits and discomforting questions were central to the work.

The questions were deliberately personal, the kind powerful people are rarely asked publicly. That discomfort, along with masks as a recurring motif, shaped the conceptual direction.

Pramit Ranjan
02
Process_

Layering identities through acetate and light, together we are one.

Layered multiple identities into single images using acetate and a custom-built A4 lightbox. Gelli printing introduced a propaganda aesthetic through paint transfer, while photograms removed context entirely and opened the figures to interpretation.

Pramit Ranjan
03
Solution_

A multi-part installation where power is constructed, projected, and concealed.

Masks, acetate lightbox prints, and gelli transfers worked together as a single installation language. Each part was less about one leader and more about how power gets assembled into an image.

Pramit Ranjan
04
Outcome_

Removing colour and context made the images feel universal. Anyone could be in power.

The strongest takeaway was that abstraction made the work more open, not less specific. The next step would be pushing the interview material further so the spoken voice becomes part of the installation itself.

Outcome_
Power stopped looking personal and started looking structural.

Once colour and immediate context were stripped away, the portraits began to read less as individuals and more as stand-ins for authority itself.

Next_
The interview component could become part of the installation.

The project has room to expand beyond image and object into sound or text, letting private responses sit beside the constructed public faces.
